Definitions and Preliminaries

Bailey pair tables

A pair \( (\alpha_n, \beta_n) \) is called a Bailey pair relative to \( x/q \) if \[ \beta_n = \sum_{r=0}^n \frac{\alpha_r}{(x;q)_{n+r} (q;q)_{n-r}} \tag{BP} .\] In each case, Eq. (BP) will be evaluated using a specialization of Bailey's sum of a very-well-poised \({}_6\psi_6\) series (Bailey, 1936); see also Gasper and Rahman (2004, p. 140 ff.) \[ \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty \frac{(1-aq^{2n})(b,c,d,e;q)_n} {(1-a)(aq/b, aq/c, aq/d, aq/e;q)_n } \Big( \frac{a^2q}{bcd} \Big)^n = \frac{(q,q/a,aq,aq/(bc),aq/(bd),aq/(be), aq/(cd), aq/(ce),aq/(de),q)_\infty} {(q/b,q/c,q/d,q/e,aq/b,aq/c,aq/d,aq/e,a^2q/(bcde);q)_\infty}, \tag{BBS}\] where \( |a^2 q / (bcde)| < 1 \) and \(|q|<1\).

Always, \( \alpha_0 = \beta_0 = 1 . \)

Historical note: Group A Bailey pairs are transformations due to Rogers (1917), translated into Bailey pair language by Slater. Group J pairs are due to Slater (1952).

Groups C, E, G, and I

In (BBS), set \( b = q^{-n/2} \), \( q^{(1-n)/2} \) and replace \( q \) by \(q^2\) throughout. This yields \[ \sum_{r = -\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} \frac{(1-aq^{4r}) (q^{-n};q)_{2r} a^{2r} q^{2nr+r} (d,e;q^2)_r} {(1-a) (aq^{n+1};q)_{2r} (aq^2/d, aq^2/e;q^2)_r (de)^r} = \frac{(q^2/q, aq/d, aq/e, aq^2/[de]; q^2)_\infty } { (q, q^2/d, q^2/e, a^2q/[de];q^2)_\infty } \frac{(q, aq, a^2q/[de];q^2)_n}{(aq;q^2)_n (aq/d,aq/d;q)_n}, \] where \(a\) has a a value such that the series terminates below at \( r = -\lfloor n/2 \rfloor \).
